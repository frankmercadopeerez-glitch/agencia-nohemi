Traveling with a Local in Cartagena: Why It Changes Everything You Thought You Knew

By Nohemi 10 min read

There are two ways to get to know Cartagena. The first is what most people do: follow the Google map, visit the spots marked in red, eat at the restaurants with the most reviews, and go home with photos of the same corners that the previous ten thousand tourists photographed. The second is to go with someone who was born here, who knows every alleyway, every cook, every hidden eatery and every story that is not in any book.

Nohemi chose to work in tourism not to sell tours, but to share her city in a way no travel app ever could.

What Google does not know about Cartagena

Google Maps is a brilliant tool for finding what has already been indexed, documented and massively reviewed. But Cartagena has layers that the algorithm cannot capture: the corozo juice vendor who only opens Tuesday through Friday on a corner in Getsemani, the cook who makes the city's best mote de queso in her own home and serves it by request, the beach the locals know just 20 minutes from the city where there are never any tourists.

This kind of information is not searched for, it is inherited. It is passed down from generation to generation among Cartagena natives who truly know their city. And that is exactly what Nohemi shares with her travelers.

The problem with "hotel guides"

Hotels in Cartagena usually have a concierge or front desk staff who recommend restaurants and tours. What many tourists do not know is that those recommendations are rarely disinterested. Hotels have commercial agreements with specific tour operators and restaurants that pay them a commission for every client referred.

The result is that you end up at the most expensive restaurant in the Centro Historico (which pays a commission to the hotel) instead of the family eatery in Manga that serves the most extraordinary seafood rice in the city. The concierge is not a bad person, they simply have their incentives. But those incentives do not necessarily align with your experience.

With Nohemi, the incentive is different. She built her business on a reputation for authentic experiences and honest recommendations. The places she takes you are the ones she herself chooses for her personal guests.

Real savings vs. the "tourist price"

There is a phenomenon known in tourism as the "tourist price": the gap between what a local pays and what the same establishment charges a visitor who does not know the alternatives. In Cartagena that gap is real, and it can be significant.

A seafood rice lunch at a tourist-oriented restaurant in the Centro Historico costs between $80,000 and $150,000 COP. The same dish, prepared with the same ingredients or better, at a restaurant in Manga or Getsemani where the neighborhood locals eat, costs $30,000-$50,000 COP.

Nohemi does not take you where you will be charged tourist prices. And when you do go somewhere more expensive, you know exactly why it is worth it.

The peace of mind of traveling with someone who knows

Beyond authenticity and savings, there is a safety factor that few people consider when planning their trip. Cartagena, like any tourist city in the world, has areas and situations where an unguided visitor can be exposed to risks that a local navigates naturally.

When you travel with Nohemi, you do not have to figure out whether the taxi that stopped is trustworthy, you do not have to guess whether the street vendor is honest, and you do not have to worry about whether the eatery you want to walk into is hygienic. She already knows. And that knowledge translates into real peace of mind to simply enjoy yourself.

The routes that exist in no guidebook

Nohemi takes her travelers to Cartagena before Cartagena wakes up: to the Bazurto Market at 7 in the morning when the fishermen are unloading the day's catch, when the prices are the ones local cooks pay and the atmosphere is 100% authentic. To the La Boquilla neighborhood where the vueltiao hat is woven by hand and where an artisan teaches the weaving technique firsthand. To a family home in Manga where on Sundays the whole family gathers to prepare sancocho costeño.

These are experiences no standard tour offers, because no conventional agency has access to them. They are the privilege of traveling with someone who belongs to the place.
